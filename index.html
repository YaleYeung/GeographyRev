Climate Change Overview

Climate Change refers to significant, long-term shifts in global or regional climate patterns:

Natural Processes

E.g., Milankovitch cycles (Earth-Sun orbital distance shifts driving historical ice ages).

Human-Induced

Anthropogenic greenhouse emissions (the dominant driver of modern warming trends).

IPCC Forecasts (21st Century)

TEMP
Increase exceeding +1.5°C globally by 2100. High latitudes hit hardest (Arctic projected up 7–11°C; equatorial changes are smaller).
RAIN
Precipitation patterns diverge. High latitudes get wetter. Mid-to-low latitudes (e.g., Mediterranean, Central America) get drier. Extreme rainfall grows more intense & frequent.

Core Differences

Phenomenon What is it? Direct Impact
Greenhouse Effect Natural thermal trapping Keeps the Earth warm enough for survival
Global Warming Rising global average temperature Driven directly by anthropogenic GHGs
Climate Change Broader environmental shifts Long-term shifts in temp, rain, and winds
FOSSIL FUELS & AGRICULTURE PAGE 2

Anthropogenic Drivers

1. Fossil Fuel Combustion

Coal, oil, and natural gas have been our primary energy sources since the 1960s. Power generation and transport are the largest sources. Air travel has the highest carbon emissions per unit of distance.

Carbon Output Comparison (g/km)

Cargo Ship 3g
Rail 18g
Truck 47g
Plane 560g

2. Farming Practices

A. Deforestation (Carbon Sinks Lost)

Clearing forests limits the planet's ability to absorb CO₂ through photosynthesis. Burning plants to clear land releases stored carbon back into the atmosphere.

B. Commercial Livestock (Methane Emission)

Cattle digestion releases methane (CH₄). Manure decomposition releases both methane and nitrous oxide (N₂O).

KEY FACT: CH₄ is 25 times more potent than CO₂ at trapping atmospheric heat.
C. Synthetic Fertilizers

The breakdown of nitrogen-based fertilizers in soil directly releases nitrous oxide into the air.

INDUSTRY & WASTE MATRICES PAGE 3

3. Industrial Activities

Cement Manufacturing

The chemical process of heating limestone to make lime releases carbon dioxide. The cement industry generates 8% of total global carbon emissions.

Aerosols \& Cooling Systems

Historically produced powerful man-made gases called chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs). CFCs absorb 4,600 times more heat than CO₂ and remain active for decades.

4. Waste Management

Landfilling and incineration both produce CO₂. In addition, landfills generate high volumes of methane during anaerobic decay.

📈 Global waste generation is projected to rise from 2 billion tonnes (2016) to 3.4 billion tonnes (2050)—a 70% increase.

CLIMATE CHANGE NOTES PAGE 4

Regional Climates

1. Hong Kong & Pearl River Delta

Hong Kong's climate is influenced by local emissions and rapid industrialization in the surrounding Pearl River Delta region.

Local Emission Trend

Hong Kong's CO₂ emissions increased 31.9-fold between 1950 and 2019.

Primary Drivers

Electricity generation (65.7%) & Transport (18.1%).

2. Urban Heat Island (UHI) Effect

Built structures absorb and trap heat, which slows down nighttime cooling. This leaves urban areas significantly warmer than rural surroundings.

Urban Core Areas

Densely built tall high-rises block wind and trap heat. Concrete surfaces absorb solar energy, and air conditioning and cars release heat.

Rural Counterparts

Low-density buildings allow heat to escape. Plants cool the surrounding air through transpiration.

3. Natural Environment Impacts

✔

Albedo Loss Feedback: Melting glaciers expose darker soil beneath, which absorbs more heat and accelerates further melting.

✔

Shrinking Sea Ice: Arctic sea ice extent shrunk by 13% per decade between 1981 and 2010.

✔

Sea Level Rise: Global sea level rise accelerated from 1.7 mm/year (1901–2010) to 3.3 mm/year (1993–2020).

Human & Global Response

Societal Threats

Water & Crops

Declining water security in dry regions. Global food prices are projected to rise by 20% by 2050 due to agricultural losses.

Health \& Capital

Expanding vector-borne disease ranges (malaria). US$ 2.2 trillion in climate hazard losses over the last two decades.

National Mitigation & Adaptation

Strategy Mainland China Hong Kong SAR
Mitigation Expanding wind, hydro, solar; closing high-emission units; countrywide afforestation. Replacing coal with natural gas and zero-carbon nuclear imports; improving green building codes.
Adaptation Building protective dams (e.g., Three Gorges) to manage severe river floods. Constructing deep drainage tunnels and underground storage reservoirs (e.g., Tai Hang Tung).

Scientific Glossary

Term Definition
Greenhouse Effect Natural process where atmospheric gases trap outgoing heat, warming the planet.
Carbon Sink Natural systems (e.g., forests) that absorb and store carbon dioxide.
Urban Heat Island A localized effect where cities are warmer than surrounding rural areas due to human activity and built structures.
Coral Bleaching The loss of symbiotic algae from coral reefs due to rising water temperatures, leaving the structures fragile.
