What is Climate Change?

Climate change refers to significant long-term changes in climatic patterns. These changes can be caused by:

  • Natural processes: (e.g., occurrence of an ice age related to cyclical changes in Earth-Sun distance)
  • Human-induced factors: currently the dominant cause of contemporary warming cycles.

Future Scenarios (IPCC Projections)

1. Temperature Changes

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) uses computer models to project climate scenarios. By the end of the 21st century, global mean temperatures are likely to increase by more than 1.5°C.

High-latitude regions may experience a much larger increase compared to tropical regions. For example, the Arctic region shows increases of 7–11°C, while equatorial regions exhibit smaller shifts.

2. Rainfall Changes

Global rainfall patterns will become more extreme with larger regional differences:

  • High-latitude regions: May receive more rainfall.
  • Mid-latitude regions: (e.g., southern Europe, Mediterranean) May receive less rainfall.
  • Low-latitude regions: (e.g., Central America, northern South America) May receive less rainfall.
  • Extreme rainfall events may become more intense and more frequent.

Human Activities

The IPCC reports that human activities are very likely the major causes of modern climate change.

1. Use of Fossil Fuels

Since the 1960s, fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas) have been our major energy source. Extraction and burning of fossil fuels emit large amounts of greenhouse gases, primarily used for power generation and transport.

"A one-way flight from New York to Berlin produces half as much carbon dioxide as an Indian produces in a year."

2. Farming Activities

a. Opening Up Farmland

Trees are important carbon sinks, absorbing CO₂ through photosynthesis. Massive clearance of forests means fewer trees to absorb excess gas. Additionally, burning vegetation to clear land releases massive amounts of CO₂ back into the atmosphere.

b. Pastoral Farming

Commercial pastoral farming emits large quantities of methane (CH₄) and nitrous oxide (N₂O). In cattle rearing, CH₄ is produced through digestion and manure decomposition. CH₄ is 25 times more potent than CO₂ in absorbing heat.

c. Chemical Fertilisers

Chemical fertilisers decompose and release nitrous oxide, increasing the warming potential of agricultural soils.

3. Industrial Activities

a. Cement Production

The cement industry is a major source of greenhouse gases. Demand has soared alongside rapid global urbanization. Cement production releases large amounts of CO₂ directly during chemical processing.

2015 Emissions Context: Cement industry operations generated roughly 8% of global carbon emissions, while total country shares placed China at 29% and the USA at 14%.

b. Refrigerant and Aerosol Production

Manufacturing of refrigerants and aerosols historically introduced chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs). These man-made gases are exceptionally powerful heat absorbers, retaining 4,600 times more heat than CO₂. They can remain in the atmosphere for up to 45 years.

4. Waste Treatment

Billions of tonnes of solid waste are generated annually. The primary disposal pathways, landfilling and incineration, both emit substantial CO₂. Furthermore, landfills produce high concentrations of methane as organic components decompose anaerobically.

Waste Forecast: Global waste production is projected to increase by 70% between 2016 and 2050 (growing from 2 billion to 3.4 billion tonnes).

Regional Climates

1. Climate Shifts in Hong Kong

Hong Kong is affected by rising local emissions as well as rapid industrial development in the surrounding Zhujiang (Pearl River) Delta region.

  • Local Emission Growth: Hong Kong's CO₂ output rose from 1.3 million tonnes in 1950 to 41.5 million tonnes in 2019—a 31.9-fold increase.
  • Power Sector Impact: Generating electricity by burning coal and natural gas accounts for 65.7% of total local emissions.
  • Transport Sector: At 18.1%, transport is the second largest emitter due to high traffic volumes and growing vehicle ownership.

2. Urban Heat Island Effect

The Urban Heat Island effect means cities are warmer than surrounding rural areas. Built structures trap heat, and built-up areas cool down more slowly. In Hong Kong, this is especially pronounced near Victoria Harbour and in densely built new towns.

Comparison: Urban vs. Rural Areas

Factors Urban Areas Rural Areas
Structural Density Tall, closely spaced buildings block wind and trap heat. Sparsely distributed buildings allow heat to disperse easily.
Surface Cover Paved concrete and asphalt absorb and store solar energy. Soil and vegetation cool the air through transpiration.
Waste Heat Air conditioners and vehicles generate high levels of waste heat. Minimal traffic and building density produce little waste heat.

3. Natural Environment Impacts

  • Melting Cryosphere: Glacier retreat exposes darker ground beneath, which absorbs more solar energy. This forms a warming feedback loop. Arctic sea ice shrank at an average rate of 13% per decade between 1981 and 2010.
  • Rising Sea Levels: Warming seawater expands, and melting glaciers add fresh water to the oceans.

Human & Global Response

Impacts on Human Societies

  • Water Shortage: Shifting rainfall patterns threaten water security, particularly in semi-arid areas.
  • Farming Declines: Longer growing seasons at high latitudes offer a short-term benefit, but droughts, storms, and pests are projected to reduce overall global crop yields in the long term.
  • Loss of Homes: Sea-level rise and severe storms threaten coastal cities and low-lying islands, creating climate refugees.
  • Health Risks: Warmer temperatures help infectious diseases spread into new areas, and heat waves pose direct risks to human health.

Mitigation and Adaptation

Strategy Mainland China Hong Kong SAR
Mitigation Expanding wind, hydro, and solar energy; closing high-emission factories; large-scale afforestation. Phasing out coal in favor of natural gas and zero-carbon nuclear imports; promoting green building codes.
Adaptation Building large-scale dykes and dams (e.g., Three Gorges Dam) to protect against major river floods. Constructing deep drainage tunnels and underground storage reservoirs (e.g., Tai Hang Tung).

Scientific Glossary

Term Scientific Definition
Greenhouse Effect A natural process where atmospheric gases trap outgoing heat, warming the planet.
Global Warming The gradual rise in average global temperatures, driven by human-induced greenhouse gas emissions.
Carbon Sink A natural system (such as a forest) that absorbs and stores carbon dioxide from the atmosphere.
Urban Heat Island A microclimatic effect where cities are warmer than surrounding rural areas due to human activity and built structures.
Coral Bleaching The loss of symbiotic algae from coral reefs due to rising sea temperatures, leaving the coral fragile.
